Part 2 · 09:30

What is this thing?

A working mental model — not an accurate one. Enough to use it well.

The one idea

It predicts language. It is not a database.

An AI language model does one thing, extremely well:

it predicts what words should come next.

It learned this by playing fill-in-the-blank, billions of times, over a huge amount of text:

USER: What produces the enzyme laccase? ASSISTANT: Laccase is commonly produced by _______
Why this matters

It is not looking anything up in a table of facts — it is generating the most plausible continuation. Because it predicts rather than retrieves, it can be fluent and confidently wrong. Everything else today follows from this one idea.

Modern AI · 2026

Not just text — multi-modal

📝 Text

Read, write, summarise, translate, reason.

🖼️ Images

Read a gel, a plate photo, a scanned datasheet, a plot.

📊 Data & files

Spreadsheets, PDFs, whole folders of papers or results.

🎙️ Voice

Talk to it; transcribe and act on a recorded meeting.

When we say "modern AI" today, we mean agentic, multi-modal AI of 2026 — models that see, read, and act, not just chat.

The most important word today

Context = everything the AI can "see" right now

Think of it as the model's working desk. Whatever is on the desk — your question, the files you gave it, the conversation so far — is what it reasons over.

Nothing else exists to it. If it's not on the desk, it doesn't know it — not your unpublished data, not last week's lab meeting, not the paper you haven't pasted in.

The desk is large but not infinite. On a long task, older items get cleared to make room.

The practical rule

Better answers come from a better desk. Put the right things in front of it — the paper, the dataset, the methods section — and be specific about what you want.

Context engineering

Garbage in, garbage out — so put good things in

Weak

Write something about my results.

Strong — Role · Task · Context · Format

You are a research assistant helping draft a methods section. Summarise the sequencing QC results below for a [labmate who will run the next step]. Structure: what was checked, what passed/failed, what to do next. Under 200 words. Here is the QC report: [PASTE]
Four parts, every time

Role — who it should be.
Task — what to do.
Context — what it needs to know.
Format — what the output should look like.

Strengths & limits

Brilliant at some things, weak at others

Great at

  • Drafting, rewriting, summarising
  • Explaining a method or a result back to you
  • Structuring messy notes into a clean protocol
  • First-pass analysis and comparison across papers
  • Doing the same task across many files

Weak / risky at

  • Exact citations, DOIs, and numbers
  • Very recent papers (unless it searches)
  • Knowing what it doesn't know
  • Your unwritten lab context
  • Anything you can't check
A concept worth knowing

"Jagged" intelligence

It can draft a discussion section in seconds — then miscount a sample size. It can explain a pathway — then invent a citation.

Hallucination

Because it predicts plausible text, it will sometimes produce a confident, well-formatted answer that is simply wrong — a fake reference, a made-up statistic. This isn't a bug you can switch off. It's how the tool works.

→ That's why verification is a theme we return to all day, and the centre of this afternoon's session. (Remember the carwash.)

Keeping honest

…and a cautionary tale about hype

In late 2025, a company claimed its AI had "solved 10 open problems" in mathematics.

It hadn't. It had done a literature search and surfaced solutions that already existed — "open" only in one outdated list. A senior figure called the overclaim "embarrassing."

Both things are true: AI is doing genuinely remarkable science and the field is full of overclaiming. Your scientific scepticism is exactly the right instinct to bring — and exactly what this afternoon's session is built around.

Part 5

From chatbot to agent

The leap that makes 2026 different: AI that doesn't just answer — it acts. Scaffolded, as we go, by ten simple rules for working with an AI research assistant.

The missing pieces

A bare model can think and talk. To be truly useful, it needs more.

🧠 Memory

To remember beyond one conversation — your project, your literature, past work.

🛠️ Tools

To act on the world — search the web, read a file, run a calculation, write a draft.

Memory, in practice

You already saw the short kind in the last demo — Claude held context within one chat, so you only said what to change. The kind that matters more day-to-day is longer: a Project remembers your literature and your context across every chat inside it, so you stop re-explaining your study each time. Watch for it in the live build, coming up.

→ Rule 2: invest in durable context and memory.

Give a language model memory and tools, and the ability to plan — and you get an agent.

What is an AI agent?

Model + tools + planning + memory

🧩

The model

The reasoning core — decides what to do.

🛠️

Tools

Web search, files, code, calculators, connectors.

🗺️

Planning

Break a goal into steps; check its own work.

🧠

Memory

Hold context across a long task and beyond.

A chatbot answers your question. An agent takes your goal, makes a plan, uses tools, and works through the steps — checking in with you.

"Tool use"

The model doesn't know everything — so it reaches for instruments

When a model needs a fact, a calculation, or a file, it can call a tool instead of guessing:

  • Needs a recent paper → web search
  • Needs an exact statistic → a calculator / code
  • Needs your dataset → read the file
Your own analogy

You don't calculate a titration in your head — you use the instrument. Tool use lets the model do the same: offload what it shouldn't guess.

→ Rule 8: know the assistant's reach, and make it flag its limits.

You already know this loop

An agent works like the scientific method

Plan

Form a hypothesis / decide the next step.

Act

Run the experiment / use a tool.

Observe

Read the result.

Revise

Adjust and go again — until done.

Plan → Act → Observe → Revise. The agent runs this loop for you, many times, quickly.

Where do agents run?

Three surfaces, same engine

Claude Chat

Converse

The chat window. Ask, attach files, search, run deep research, use Projects & connectors.

Best for: everyday questions, drafting, quick analysis.

Claude Cowork

Delegate

Point it at a folder or task; it works across files and produces deliverables while you steer.

Best for: multi-step research productivity and analysis.

Claude Code

Automate

An agent in your computer's terminal — reads & writes files, runs commands, builds pipelines.

Best for: data, automation, technical workflows.

The escalation ladder

Climb only as high as the task needs

1
Chat — one-off questions & drafts. "Summarise this paper."
2
Projects — reusable context. A "Literature Assistant" that knows your reading list.
3
Cowork — multi-step work across files. Dataset → summary → chart → draft section.
4
Agents — recurring, autonomous work. A weekly scan that flags new papers in your field.

Rule of thumb: start at the lowest rung that does the job. Climb when the task repeats or spans many steps.

→ Rule 1: give the assistant a workspace it can navigate. Rule 10: encode what works into a reusable template.

Part 6 · 13:00

Scientific rigor & security

The habits that let us confidently say yes to AI in research: is it true, and is it safe.

The reframe

Ten simple rules for working with an AI research assistant

  1. Give it a workspace it can navigate
  2. Invest in durable context & memory
  3. Build a living knowledge base, not one-off answers
  4. Delegate the tedious, own the science
  5. Let one assistant span the whole research lifecycle
  1. Verify against authoritative sources, systematically
  2. Track provenance; tier sources by authority
  3. Know the assistant's reach — make it flag its limits
  4. Version the AI-assisted workflow itself
  5. Encode what works into a reusable, shared template

You've already seen most of these today. This afternoon is rules 6 and 7 — verification and provenance — in depth.

Before you paste

Five questions, every time

  1. Confidentiality — am I about to share something I shouldn't?
  2. Data protection — is there personal or participant data in here?
  3. Intellectual property — is this ours to share, and unpublished?
  4. Bias & fairness — could the answer misrepresent our context?
  5. Verification — how will I check this before I trust it?
The Proportionality Principle

A jet, a car, and a bicycle

🚲 Bicycle

Manual methods. Slow, but the failure modes are small and visible.

🚗 Car

Previous digital tools. Faster, with known, bounded risks.

✈️ Jet

AI. Enormous capability — and enormously more can go wrong, faster, if mishandled.

Safeguards must scale with capability. AI is most dangerous exactly where you lack domain experience to catch its mistakes — and, used to learn rather than to substitute your judgement, the same power is a learning accelerant.

The research data traffic light

What can I paste?

Your settings decide retention and training use — you checked this at 09:15. That's what makes amber genuinely safer. It doesn't change red.

Green — go

Published papers, public datasets, your own non-sensitive drafts. Any assistant.

Amber — care

Your own unpublished manuscript drafts, preliminary results, internal reports. Safe on a correctly configured account. When in doubt, treat as red.

Red — stop

Participant/personal data, a collaborator's unpublished data without permission, embargoed results, confidential grant reviews. Settings don't change this — you need a proper legal/ethical basis to share it with any processor.

Rule of thumb: your settings decide how long it's kept and whether it trains the model. The content decides whether it should leave your hands at all.

The habit that matters most

Verify everything — the anti-fabrication habit

Numbers, quotes, and citations get checked against the real source — every time. This is already policy in serious research projects:

"This is a scientific project. Accuracy and precision is of the utmost importance. Express uncertainty, rather than inventing or being over confident. […] All claims need to be verified, or labelled as uncertain."

AI for drafting. Humans and primary sources for truth. Treat an AI summary as a map, not the territory.

Standardise it — Rule 6

Put a version of that quoted policy as a standing instruction in a Project, CLAUDE.md, or Skill. This raises the floor — it doesn't replace spot-checking, because an agent can still claim to have verified something it didn't.

The single biggest reliability lever

Ground it — make it work from the real text

Ungrounded (risky)

What filtering thresholds does this type of pipeline usually use?

Grounded (reliable)

Using ONLY the methods section pasted below, what filtering thresholds were used? If it's not stated, say "not specified." [PASTE THE METHODS SECTION]
Why it works

Grounding removes the model's room to invent. It can only answer from what you gave it — and it will tell you when the answer isn't there.

Know the ground you stand on

The regulatory & institutional context

POPIA

South Africa's Protection of Personal Information Act — the Information Regulator oversees lawful processing of personal data.

Your ethics & data policy

UP's research ethics committee and data management plan requirements apply to AI tools exactly as they do to any other processor.

Journal & funder rules

Many journals and funders now require disclosing AI use — check before you submit, not after.

Orientation, not legal advice — confirm specifics with your ethics committee and supervisor, and check each journal's and funder's requirements too.

A special note for African science

The model's blind spots are often your context

These models learned mostly from wealthy, English-dominant data. They can under-represent:

Local pathogens, crops, and agro-ecologies

African fungal/plant taxonomy and place-specific knowledge

Regional journals and grey literature, less indexed

Indigenous and local ecological knowledge

So: give local context explicitly, and have a specialist colleague check technical terms it may not know well.

✋ Red-team · spot the problem

What's wrong here?

1. "The AI gave me three references for my introduction." → Open all three in a real database first. Unread ≠ real.

2. "It summarised a 40-page protocol for my methods section." → Ground it in the text, constrain it, and spot-check against the original.

3. "It calculated my diversity index / fold-change." → Have it compute in a script or notebook showing its working — never head-math into a manuscript.
