Part 1 · 09:15

What is this thing?

A working mental model — not an accurate one. Enough to use it well.

The one idea

It predicts language. It is not a database.

An AI language model does one thing, extremely well:

it predicts what words should come next.

It is not looking anything up in a table of facts. It is generating the most plausible continuation, one piece at a time.

Why this matters

Because it predicts rather than retrieves, it can be fluent and confidently wrong. Everything else today follows from this one idea.

What is an LLM?

"Fill in the blank," at enormous scale

A Large Language Model (LLM) learned by playing fill-in-the-blank over a huge amount of text:

USER: What is the capital of France? ASSISTANT: The capital of France is _______

Do that billions of times, and it gets astonishingly good at continuing text sensibly — answering questions, drafting, translating, summarising. A chat is just this: the model predicting the assistant's next words.

How it was built

Most of the cost is behind you. The part you touch is cheap.

$$$$
Pre-training — read most of the internet. Done once, by the vendor.
$$$
Fine-tuning — learn to follow instructions and be helpful.
$$
Alignment — learn to be safe and honest.
$
Your prompt — the instructions you give it. This is the layer you control.

You don't need to build a model. You need to get good at the last, cheap layer: talking to it well.

Modern AI · July 2026

Not just text — multi-modal

📝 Text

Read, write, summarise, translate, reason.

🖼️ Images

Read a chart, a scanned form, a photo of a diseased leaf.

📊 Data & files

Spreadsheets, PDFs, whole folders of documents.

🎙️ Voice

Talk to it; transcribe and act on recordings.

When we say "modern AI" today, we mean agentic, multi-modal AI of 2026 — models that see, read, and act, not just chat.

The most important word today

Context = everything the AI can "see" right now

Think of it as the model's working desk. Whatever is on the desk — your question, the files you gave it, the conversation so far — is what it reasons over.

Nothing else exists to it. If it's not on the desk, it doesn't know it.

The desk is large but not infinite. On a big task, older items get cleared to make room.

The practical rule

Better answers come from a better desk. Put the right things in front of it — the document, the data, the example — and be specific about what you want.

Context engineering

Garbage in, garbage out — so put good things in

Weak

Write something about maize.

Strong — Role · Task · Context · Format

You are a proposal writer for AATF. Draft a 1-page concept note on [TELA maize scale-up] for [Donor X], who funds [climate resilience]. Structure: Problem, Solution, Impact, Why AATF, Budget. Under 500 words. Here is our background: [PASTE]
Four parts, every time

Role — who it should be.
Task — what to do.
Context — what it needs to know.
Format — what the output should look like.

Strengths & limits

Brilliant at some things, weak at others

Great at

  • Drafting, rewriting, summarising
  • Translating & changing tone
  • Explaining and structuring messy notes
  • First-pass analysis and comparison
  • Doing the same task across many files

Weak / risky at

  • Facts, numbers, and exact citations
  • Very recent events (unless it searches)
  • Knowing what it doesn't know
  • Your local, unwritten context
  • Anything you can't check
A concept worth knowing

"Jagged" intelligence

It can draft a funding proposal in seconds — then miscount the words. It can explain genomics — then invent a citation.

Hallucination

Because it predicts plausible text, it will sometimes produce a confident, well-formatted answer that is simply wrong — a fake reference, a made-up number. This isn't a bug you can switch off. It's how the tool works.

→ That's why verification is a theme we return to all day. (Remember the carwash.)

A capable assistant, not an oracle.
It amplifies your judgement. It does not replace it.

Is any of this actually useful?

What scientists — including in agriculture and Africa — have already done.

2024

AI was at the centre of two Nobel Prizes in one year

Chemistry 2024

Protein structure & design

Demis Hassabis & John Jumper (AlphaFold), and David Baker. AI cracked a 50-year grand-challenge in biology.

Physics 2024

The foundations of machine learning

John Hopfield & Geoffrey Hinton, for the neural-network ideas that made all of this possible.

This is not a fringe technology. The scientific establishment has recognised it at the highest level.

A problem that defined careers is now a free lookup. That is the scale of change we're talking about.

2025 · the "AI co-scientist"

A decade of work, reproduced in two days

Google's multi-agent "AI co-scientist" was given a hard open question about how superbugs acquire antibiotic resistance.

In ~2 days, it independently proposed the same key hypothesis a research team had spent ~10 years reaching — a result they had not yet published. The lead confirmed the AI had no access to their unpublished work.

AI is starting to help generate hypotheses, not just process data — the front end of science.

2025 · mathematics

Gold-medal reasoning

At the 2025 International Mathematical Olympiad, an advanced AI model solved 5 of 6 problems — a gold-medal standard, graded under the same rules as the students.

For comparison: only 67 of 630 human contestants earned gold that year.

Why care?

These aren't lookup tasks. They need multi-step reasoning — the same muscle behind analysing a trial, structuring an argument, or checking a budget.

Closest to home · agriculture in Africa

A crop doctor in every pocket

PlantVillage Nuru ("nuru" = light in Swahili) diagnoses crop disease from a phone photo — cassava mosaic and brown-streak disease, fall armyworm on maize.

  • Runs fully offline — built for low-connectivity rural areas.
  • In field tests, roughly twice as accurate as the human extension officers it was compared against.
The point

This is not Silicon Valley solving Silicon Valley's problems. It's smallholder farmers, African crops, real constraints.

Keeping honest

…and a cautionary tale about hype

In late 2025, a company claimed its AI had "solved 10 open problems" in mathematics.

It hadn't. It had done a literature search and surfaced solutions that already existed — "open" only in one outdated list. A senior figure called the overclaim "embarrassing."

Both things are true: AI is doing genuinely remarkable science and the field is full of overclaiming. Your scientific scepticism is exactly the right instinct to bring.

So — for AATF

The constraint on an R&D foundation is rarely ideas. It's skilled time.

AI reclaims hours from routine cognitive work — drafting, summarising, reformatting, first-pass analysis — and hands them back for judgement, relationships, and science.

More output, higher quality, from the same team.

Part 4 · 11:00

From chatbot to agent

The leap that makes 2026 different: AI that doesn't just answer — it acts.

The missing pieces

A bare model can think and talk. To be truly useful, it needs more.

🧠 Memory

To remember beyond one conversation — your project, your preferences, past work.

🛠️ Tools

To act on the world — search the web, read a file, run a calculation, send a draft.

Give a language model memory and tools, and the ability to plan — and you get an agent.

What is an AI agent?

Model + tools + planning + memory

🧩

The model

The reasoning core — decides what to do.

🛠️

Tools

Web search, files, code, calculators, connectors.

🗺️

Planning

Break a goal into steps; check its own work.

🧠

Memory

Hold context across a long task and beyond.

A chatbot answers your question. An agent takes your goal, makes a plan, uses tools, and works through the steps — checking in with you.

"Tool use"

The model doesn't know everything — so it reaches for instruments

When a model needs a fact, a calculation, or a file, it can call a tool instead of guessing:

  • Needs today's news → web search
  • Needs an exact sum → a calculator / code
  • Needs your report → read the file
Scientist's analogy

You don't calculate a titration in your head — you use the instrument. Tool use lets the model do the same: offload what it shouldn't guess.

You already know this loop

An agent works like the scientific method

Plan

Form a hypothesis / decide the next step.

Act

Run the experiment / use a tool.

Observe

Read the result.

Revise

Adjust and go again — until done.

Plan → Act → Observe → Revise. The agent runs this loop for you, many times, quickly.

Where do agents run?

Three surfaces, same engine

Claude Chat

Converse

The chat window. Ask, attach files, search, run deep research, use Projects & connectors.

Best for: everyday questions, drafting, quick analysis.

Claude Cowork

Delegate

Point it at a folder or task; it works across files and produces deliverables while you steer.

Best for: multi-step productivity, research, analysis.

Claude Code

Automate

An agent in your computer's terminal — reads & writes files, runs commands, builds pipelines.

Best for: data, automation, technical workflows.

The escalation ladder

Climb only as high as the task needs

1
Chat — one-off questions & drafts. "Summarise this report."
2
Projects — reusable context. A "Donor Proposal Assistant" that knows AATF.
3
Cowork — multi-step work across files. Report → summary → chart → brief.
4
Agents — recurring, autonomous work. A weekly funding-scan that drafts an email.

Rule of thumb: start at the lowest rung that does the job. Climb when the task repeats or spans many steps.

Using it responsibly

The rules that let us confidently say yes to AI.

The reframe

You already have the discipline. Point it at AI.

Your research habitBecomes your AI habit
Never cite a paper you haven't readNever send a citation the AI gave you unread
Work from primary dataGround the AI in the real source, not its memory
Cross-check surprising resultsVerify surprising AI claims a second way
Peer review before it goes outA human signs off before it leaves AATF

Making AI reliable isn't a new skill for AATF. It's your existing standards, applied to a new tool.

Before you paste

Five questions, every time

  1. Confidentiality — am I about to share something I shouldn't?
  2. Data protection — is there personal data in here?
  3. Intellectual property — is this ours to share, and unpublished?
  4. Bias & fairness — could the answer misrepresent our context?
  5. Verification — how will I check this before I trust it?
The AATF data traffic light

What can I paste?

Green — go

Public/published info, general drafting, your own non-sensitive text. Any assistant.

Amber — care

Internal drafts, partner material, early proposals. De-identify; prefer enterprise accounts. When in doubt, treat as red.

Red — stop

Personal/HR/medical data, confidential donor/legal material, unpublished IP. Approved tools only — or not at all.

One-line rule: if you'd hesitate to email it to a stranger, don't paste it into a public AI.

The habit that matters most

Verify everything that leaves AATF

Numbers, quotes, and citations get checked against the real source — every time.

"A citation you haven't read is not evidence — whether a human or an AI produced it."

AI for drafting. Humans and primary sources for truth. Treat an AI summary as a map, not the territory.

The single biggest reliability lever

Ground it — make it work from the real text

Ungrounded (risky)

What does Kenya's Biosafety Act say about field trials?

Grounded (reliable)

Using ONLY the Act pasted below, what are the field-trial requirements? If it's not in the text, say "not specified." [PASTE THE ACT]
Why it works

Grounding removes the model's room to invent. It can only answer from what you gave it — and it will tell you when the answer isn't there.

The Kenyan & African context

Know the ground you stand on

Data Protection Act 2019

Kenya's ODPC oversees lawful processing, data rights, and limits on fully-automated decisions.

National AI Strategy 2025–2030

Plus a draft AI Code of Practice — data sovereignty and responsible innovation.

KEBS KS 3007:2025

Kenya's code of practice for AI applications.

Orientation, not legal advice — confirm specifics with AATF's data-protection lead and counsel, and check each donor's data requirements too.

A special note for African science

The model's blind spots are often your context

These models learned mostly from wealthy, English-dominant data. They can under-represent:

Local crops, farming systems, and agro-ecologies

African languages, names, and place-specific knowledge

Gender dynamics in smallholder farming

Regional regulation and market realities

So: give local context explicitly, and have a fluent colleague check technical French/Swahili terms.

✋ Red-team · spot the problem

What's wrong here?

1. "The AI gave me three journal references for the proposal." → Open all three in a real database first. Unread ≠ real.

2. "It summarised a 40-page regulation for our filing." → Ground it in the text, constrain it, and spot-check against the original.

3. "It calculated our pipeline conversion rate." → Have it compute in a spreadsheet showing its working — never head-math into a donor doc.
