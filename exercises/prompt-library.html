AI Prompt Library

A working reference for African agricultural scientists · Claude · July 2026

This is your keep-and-reach-for reference. It is not a script to memorise — it is a shelf of tested starting points. Copy a prompt, swap the [BLUE PLACEHOLDERS] for your real details, and refine from there. The AI does the first draft; you stay the expert who checks it.

The anatomy of a good prompt

Nearly every strong prompt has four parts. Keep them in this order and you rarely go wrong:

ROLE

Who the AI should be. "You are a grants officer…"

TASK

The one thing to do. "Draft a two-page concept note."

CONTEXT

The facts it needs. "For TELA Maize in Nigeria; donor is…"

FORMAT

Shape of the answer. "Bullet points, under 300 words."

The universal template — start here when nothing else fits
You are [ROLE, e.g. AN AATF GRANTS OFFICER]. Your task: [THE ONE THING YOU WANT DONE]. Context you need to know: - [FACT 1 — project, crop, country] - [FACT 2 — audience, deadline, constraint] - [FACT 3 — anything else that matters] Format the answer as [LENGTH / STRUCTURE / TONE]. If anything is unclear, ask me before you start.
Iterate — the first answer is a draft, not the deliverable

You do not need a perfect prompt. Say what you want changed, in plain language, and keep going:

Make it shorter Make it more formal Now in French Explain it for a non-scientist Ask me 5 questions first, then redraft

1 Resource Mobilization & Sustainability

Concept notes, donor-tailored language, donor intelligence, budgets and pipeline summaries. Always verify donor facts before they leave the building.

Turn a rough idea into a fundable two-page concept note
You are a resource-mobilization officer at AATF. Draft a 2-page concept note for [PROJECT, e.g. SCALING PBR COWPEA IN NORTHERN NIGERIA]. Context: the problem is [PROBLEM], our solution is [SOLUTION], the target donor is [DONOR] whose priorities are [PRIORITIES]. Use this structure: Problem · Our Approach · Expected Results · Budget Summary · Why AATF. Keep it plain, evidence-led and under 900 words. Flag anywhere I still need to insert a real figure.
Re-tailor the same project boilerplate for a specific donor's language
Here is our standard project description: [PASTE BOILERPLATE] Rewrite it to match the priorities and vocabulary of [DONOR], who funds [THEIR FOCUS AREAS]. Keep every fact identical — change only framing and emphasis. Show the tailored version, then a short bullet list of what you changed and why.
Build a first-pass donor profile — then check it hard
Act as a donor-intelligence analyst. Give me a briefing on [DONOR / FOUNDATION] for an agricultural-biotech proposal: funding priorities, typical grant size, geographies, application cycle and recent African ag grants. At the end, add a section titled "FACTS I MUST INDEPENDENTLY VERIFY" listing every claim, number and date you are not fully certain about.
Verify donor facts

AI can be confidently wrong about grant sizes, deadlines and past awards. Treat the profile as a research lead, never as the source of truth. Confirm on the donor's own website.

Explain a budget so a non-finance reviewer understands it
You are a grants-budget writer. Here is our budget table: [PASTE FIGURES OR ATTACH]. Write a clear budget narrative that justifies each major line for [DONOR]. Explain what each cost buys and how it links to results. Do not change any number. If a line looks unusually high or low, flag it for me instead of guessing a justification.
Summarise the funding pipeline for a board or leadership update
Summarise this pipeline data into a board-ready update: [PASTE PIPELINE / STATUS LIST]. Give me: total value in the pipeline, split by stage (prospect / submitted / awarded), the 3 biggest opportunities, and 2 risks. One short paragraph plus a table. Mark anything where the data is incomplete as "TBC" — do not fill gaps with assumptions.

2 Communications, Advocacy & Partnerships

Press releases, multi-audience messaging, speeches, policy briefs and EN⇄FR translation for West Africa.

Draft a press release from a handful of facts
You are AATF's communications officer. Write a press release announcing [MILESTONE, e.g. TELA MAIZE VARIETY RELEASED IN KENYA]. Facts: [WHO / WHAT / WHERE / WHEN / WHY IT MATTERS]. Include a headline, dateline, two quote placeholders (one AATF, one partner), and boilerplate. Newswire style, ~400 words. Leave [QUOTE] brackets where I must add real quotes.
Say one message three ways for three audiences
Take this core message: [e.g. PBR COWPEA CUTS PESTICIDE SPRAYS AND RAISES YIELDS]. Write three versions of it: 1. A LinkedIn post for partners and funders (professional, 1 short paragraph + a stat). 2. A message for smallholder farmers (simple, concrete, benefit-first, no jargon). 3. A briefing line for a Minister of Agriculture (policy impact, national relevance). Keep the facts identical across all three.
Draft speaking notes for a leader at an event
Write a 3-minute speech for [SPEAKER & ROLE] to open [EVENT]. Themes to hit: [2–3 KEY POINTS]. Audience: [WHO IS IN THE ROOM]. Warm and confident, spoken-word rhythm (short sentences), one memorable closing line. Mark [PAUSE] where the speaker should slow down.
Structure a policy brief that decision-makers actually read
You are a policy analyst. Draft a 2-page policy brief on [ISSUE, e.g. HARMONISING BIOSAFETY RULES FOR STRIGA-CONTROL TECH IN ECOWAS]. Use exactly this structure: Issue · Evidence · Options · Recommendation. Keep evidence tied to sources I will supply — do not invent statistics. Neutral, decision-focused tone. End with a one-sentence call to action.
Translate between English and French and flag the tricky calls
Translate the following from [ENGLISH] to [FRENCH] for a West African audience (target country: [e.g. BURKINA FASO]): [PASTE TEXT] Keep technical agricultural terms accurate. After the translation, add a short list titled "JUDGEMENT CALLS" flagging any term or phrase where you had to choose between options, so a native speaker can confirm.

3 Research & Product Development

Summarise papers for mixed audiences, compare trial reports, explain concepts, and tidy messy notes — without letting the AI overstate or invent.

Summarise a technical paper for a mixed audience
Summarise this paper for a mixed audience of scientists and non-scientists: [PASTE ABSTRACT / TEXT OR ATTACH]. Give me: (1) a one-line takeaway, (2) key findings in 4 bullets, (3) what it means for [e.g. TELA MAIZE DEPLOYMENT], (4) limitations. Do not overstate certainty — where the authors hedge, you hedge too.
Compare several trial or field reports side by side
Compare these trial reports and put the results in one table: [PASTE / ATTACH REPORTS]. Columns: site · season · variety · yield · pest/disease pressure · key observation. After the table, note where methods differ enough that direct comparison is unfair. Use only numbers present in the reports — leave a cell blank if a value is missing.
Explain a hard concept with one clean analogy
Explain [CONCEPT, e.g. HOW Bt INSECT RESISTANCE WORKS IN TELA MAIZE] to [AUDIENCE, e.g. OUR BOARD MEMBERS WHO ARE NOT SCIENTISTS]. Use one clear everyday analogy — just one, carried all the way through. Under 200 words. No jargon unless you define it in the same sentence.
Turn messy field notes into a clean structure
Organise these rough notes into a clean structure: [PASTE MESSY NOTES]. Group related points under clear headings and fix grammar. Do not add any facts, figures or conclusions I did not write — if something is unclear or missing, list it under "NEEDS CLARIFICATION" instead of guessing.
Chat assistants can invent citations

AI models sometimes fabricate references, DOIs and quotes that look completely real. Never cite a source the AI gave you without opening it yourself. Treat every AI summary as a map, not the territory — useful for orientation, checked against the real ground before you act.

4 Regulatory, Deployment & Commercialization

Biosafety comparisons, submission checklists, first-draft legal documents and market scans — always marked as drafts for expert review.

Compare regulatory requirements across countries
Compare the biosafety approval requirements for [PRODUCT, e.g. A GM MAIZE EVENT] across [COUNTRY A], [COUNTRY B] and [COUNTRY C]. Table format: authority · key data required · typical timeline · public-consultation step. End with a list titled "CONFIRM WITH A LOCAL REGULATORY EXPERT" for every point that may be out of date or country-specific. Do not present timelines as guarantees.
Build a submission checklist so nothing is missed
You are a regulatory-affairs officer. Create a submission checklist for [APPLICATION TYPE, e.g. CONFINED FIELD TRIAL PERMIT] in [COUNTRY]. List every document, form and annex typically required, grouped by category, with a checkbox for each. Add a final row: "verified against current regulator guidance on [DATE] by [NAME]".
Get a first draft of a term sheet or MoU to react to
Draft a first-draft [TERM SHEET / MoU] between AATF and [PARTNER] for [PURPOSE, e.g. LICENSING CASSAVA PLANTING MATERIAL]. Cover: parties, purpose, roles, IP, term, and termination. At the very top add, in bold: "UNREVIEWED DRAFT — FOR INTERNAL DISCUSSION AND LEGAL REVIEW ONLY." Use plain language and mark anything commercially sensitive as [TO BE NEGOTIATED].
Scan a market or potential partner landscape
Act as a commercialization analyst. Give me a market scan for [PRODUCT / CROP] in [COUNTRY / REGION]: demand drivers, key players, seed-system channels, and likely partners. Be explicit about what you don't know — put uncertain or unverifiable points under "LOW CONFIDENCE — VERIFY" rather than stating them as fact.

5 Project Management, M&E, Reporting & Operations

Meeting minutes, donor reports, first-pass analysis and status updates — with owners, dates and reasoning you can actually check.

Turn a meeting transcript into minutes, decisions and actions
From this meeting transcript, produce three things: [PASTE TRANSCRIPT] 1. Concise minutes (key discussion points). 2. A list of decisions made. 3. An action table: Action · Owner · Due date. Do not invent owners or dates — if the transcript does not say who or when, write "TBC".
Draft a donor report section from your data
You are an M&E officer. Write the "Progress Against Targets" section of a donor report for [PROJECT] using this data: [PASTE INDICATORS / RESULTS]. For each indicator: target, actual, and a one-line explanation of variance. Use only the figures provided. Flag any indicator with missing data as "data pending".
Get a first-pass read on a dataset — with reasoning shown
Do a first-pass analysis of this data: [PASTE DATA / ATTACH]. Tell me the main patterns, any outliers, and 3 questions worth investigating further. Show your reasoning step by step so I can check how you reached each conclusion. Do not treat correlation as cause. This is a first look, not a final analysis.
Write a weekly status report from bullet updates
Turn these rough updates into a weekly status report for [PROJECT / TEAM]: [PASTE BULLETS]. Sections: Done this week · In progress · Blockers · Next week. Keep it tight — a manager should read it in under a minute. Flag blockers clearly.
Standardise reports that everyone formats differently
Here are field reports from three teams in different formats: [PASTE / ATTACH]. Rewrite all of them into this single standard template: [PASTE YOUR TEMPLATE HEADINGS]. Preserve every fact and figure exactly. Where a team omitted a required field, write "Not reported".

6 Knowledge Management & Everyday Productivity

Fast document triage, email drafting, onboarding help, and getting unstuck when you don't know how to start.

Get the "so what do I do?" from a long document
Read this document and give me an "act on this" summary: [PASTE / ATTACH]. Return four short sections: Executive summary (3 lines) · Decisions I need to make · Deadlines & dates · Questions I should ask. Skip everything that needs no action from me.
Draft the same email in two tones so you can pick
Draft an email to [RECIPIENT] about [SUBJECT / GOAL]. Key points: [WHAT MUST BE SAID]. Give me two versions: (A) warm and relationship-first, (B) brief and direct. Keep both under 150 words with a clear subject line and one specific ask.
Create onboarding FAQs from your own material
Using this material, write an onboarding FAQ for a new [ROLE, e.g. PROJECT OFFICER] at AATF: [PASTE POLICIES / NOTES]. 10–12 question-and-answer pairs a new joiner would actually ask, plain language. Only use information in the material — mark anything you cannot answer as "Ask your manager".
When you're stuck and don't even know what to ask
I need to [TASK] but I'm stuck. Ask me 5 questions that would help you help me, then wait for my answers.
This one prompt fixes most "blank page" moments

Instead of forcing a bad first prompt, let the AI interview you. Answer its questions and the draft it produces next will be far closer to what you actually need.

Card F

Check before you trust

The card you use before anything leaves your desk.

List every factual claim, number and citation in your answer, and tell me which I must independently verify and which you're confident about.
Before you hit send: the verification habit

AI gives you speed. You provide the judgement. Every number, name, citation, deadline and legal or regulatory claim is yours to confirm before it reaches a donor, a partner, a regulator or a farmer. Use Card F on anything that matters, mark AI drafts as drafts, and never let a confident-sounding sentence replace a fact you can check.
