The anatomy of a good prompt

Nearly every strong prompt has four parts. Keep them in this order and you rarely go wrong:

ROLE

Who the AI should be. "You are a research communicator…"

TASK

The one thing to do. "Draft a methods section paragraph."

CONTEXT

The facts it needs. "For a greenhouse trial; treatment vs control…"

FORMAT

Shape of the answer. "Bullet points, under 300 words."

The universal template — start here when nothing else fits
You are [ROLE, e.g. A RESEARCH COMMUNICATOR]. Your task: [THE ONE THING YOU WANT DONE]. Context you need to know: - [FACT 1 — study system, question, or dataset] - [FACT 2 — audience, deadline, constraint] - [FACT 3 — anything else that matters] Format the answer as [LENGTH / STRUCTURE / TONE]. If anything is unclear, ask me before you start.
Iterate — the first answer is a draft, not the deliverable

You do not need a perfect prompt. Say what you want changed, in plain language, and keep going:

Make it shorter Make it more formal Now for a lay audience Explain it for a non-specialist Ask me 5 questions first, then redraft

1 Grants & Manuscripts

Concept notes, reviewer-tailored language, funder intelligence, budget narratives and pipeline summaries. Always verify funder facts before they leave your desk.

Turn a rough idea into a fundable two-page concept note
You are a research grant writer. Draft a 2-page concept note for [PROJECT, e.g. A MULTI-SITE TRIAL OF A BIOCONTROL SEED TREATMENT]. Context: the problem is [PROBLEM], our approach is [APPROACH], the target funder is [FUNDER] whose priorities are [PRIORITIES]. Use this structure: Problem · Our Approach · Expected Results · Budget Summary · Why Us. Keep it plain, evidence-led and under 900 words. Flag anywhere I still need to insert a real figure.
Re-tailor the same project description for a specific funder's language
Here is our standard project description: [PASTE BOILERPLATE] Rewrite it to match the priorities and vocabulary of [FUNDER], who funds [THEIR FOCUS AREAS]. Keep every fact identical — change only framing and emphasis. Show the tailored version, then a short bullet list of what you changed and why.
Build a first-pass funder profile — then check it hard
Act as a funding-intelligence analyst. Give me a briefing on [FUNDER / FOUNDATION] for a research proposal: funding priorities, typical grant size, eligible geographies, application cycle and recent related awards. At the end, add a section titled "FACTS I MUST INDEPENDENTLY VERIFY" listing every claim, number and date you are not fully certain about.
Verify funder facts

AI can be confidently wrong about grant sizes, deadlines and past awards. Treat the profile as a research lead, never as the source of truth. Confirm on the funder's own website.

Explain a budget so a non-specialist reviewer understands it
You are a grants-budget writer. Here is our budget table: [PASTE FIGURES OR ATTACH]. Write a clear budget narrative that justifies each major line for [FUNDER]. Explain what each cost buys and how it links to the research plan. Do not change any number. If a line looks unusually high or low, flag it for me instead of guessing a justification.
Draft a reviewer-response letter
Here are the reviewer comments on our manuscript: [PASTE COMMENTS]. For each comment, draft: (1) a short, respectful acknowledgement, (2) a plain-language summary of how we responded, (3) a pointer to where the change is in the manuscript. Do not concede a point that isn't actually correct — flag any comment where you think we should push back, rather than agreeing by default.

2 Communication & Outreach

Press releases, multi-audience messaging, talks, policy-relevant briefs and plain-language translation for non-specialists.

Draft a press release from a handful of facts
You are a research communications officer. Write a press release announcing [MILESTONE, e.g. A NEW FINDING ON DISEASE RESISTANCE]. Facts: [WHO / WHAT / WHERE / WHEN / WHY IT MATTERS]. Include a headline, dateline, two quote placeholders (one researcher, one partner), and boilerplate. Newswire style, ~400 words. Leave [QUOTE] brackets where I must add real quotes.
Say one finding three ways for three audiences
Take this core finding: [e.g. THE TREATMENT REDUCED DISEASE SEVERITY BY A THIRD]. Write three versions of it: 1. A LinkedIn post for peers and collaborators (professional, 1 short paragraph + a stat). 2. A message for a general/lay audience (simple, concrete, benefit-first, no jargon). 3. A briefing line for a policymaker or funder (why it matters, relevance to their priorities). Keep the facts identical across all three.
Draft speaking notes for a talk or symposium opening
Write a 3-minute talk opening for [SPEAKER & ROLE] to introduce [TOPIC / SESSION]. Themes to hit: [2–3 KEY POINTS]. Audience: [WHO IS IN THE ROOM]. Warm and confident, spoken-word rhythm (short sentences), one memorable closing line. Mark [PAUSE] where the speaker should slow down.
Structure a policy-relevant brief that decision-makers actually read
You are a science-policy analyst. Draft a 2-page brief on [ISSUE, e.g. IMPLICATIONS OF THIS FINDING FOR EXTENSION ADVICE]. Use exactly this structure: Issue · Evidence · Options · Recommendation. Keep evidence tied to sources I will supply — do not invent statistics. Neutral, decision-focused tone. End with a one-sentence call to action.
Translate jargon into plain language, and flag the tricky calls
Rewrite the following for a non-specialist reader with no background in [FIELD, e.g. PLANT PATHOLOGY]: [PASTE TEXT] Keep every claim accurate — simplify the language, not the substance. After the rewrite, add a short list titled "SIMPLIFICATIONS I MADE" flagging any nuance you dropped, so I can check whether it still matters for this audience.

3 Literature & Synthesis

Summarise papers for mixed audiences, compare studies, explain concepts, and tidy messy notes — without letting the AI overstate or invent.

Summarise a technical paper for a mixed audience
Summarise this paper for a mixed audience of specialists and non-specialists: [PASTE ABSTRACT / TEXT OR ATTACH]. Give me: (1) a one-line takeaway, (2) key findings in 4 bullets, (3) what it means for [e.g. OUR OWN STUDY SYSTEM], (4) limitations. Do not overstate certainty — where the authors hedge, you hedge too.
Compare several studies or trial reports side by side
Compare these studies and put the results in one table: [PASTE / ATTACH PAPERS OR REPORTS]. Columns: study · system · method · key result · sample size · key caveat. After the table, note where methods differ enough that direct comparison is unfair. Use only numbers present in the sources — leave a cell blank if a value is missing.
Explain a hard concept with one clean analogy
Explain [CONCEPT, e.g. HOW INDUCED SYSTEMIC RESISTANCE WORKS] to [AUDIENCE, e.g. A FIRST-YEAR HONOURS STUDENT]. Use one clear everyday analogy — just one, carried all the way through. Under 200 words. No jargon unless you define it in the same sentence.
Turn messy field or lab notes into a clean structure
Organise these rough notes into a clean structure: [PASTE MESSY NOTES]. Group related points under clear headings and fix grammar. Do not add any facts, figures or conclusions I did not write — if something is unclear or missing, list it under "NEEDS CLARIFICATION" instead of guessing.
Chat assistants can invent citations

AI models sometimes fabricate references, DOIs and quotes that look completely real. Never cite a source the AI gave you without opening it yourself. Treat every AI summary as a map, not the territory — useful for orientation, checked against the real ground before you act.

4 Protocols, Ethics & Reproducibility

Comparing requirements across institutions, submission checklists, first-draft agreements and method landscape scans — always marked as drafts for expert review.

Compare ethics or biosafety requirements across institutions
Compare the ethics/biosafety approval requirements for [STUDY TYPE, e.g. A FIELD TRIAL INVOLVING A GM VARIETY] across [INSTITUTION/COUNTRY A], [B] and [C]. Table format: committee/authority · key documents required · typical timeline · public-consultation step. End with a list titled "CONFIRM WITH YOUR ETHICS/BIOSAFETY OFFICE" for every point that may be out of date or institution-specific. Do not present timelines as guarantees.
Build a submission checklist so nothing is missed
You are a research-compliance officer. Create a submission checklist for [APPLICATION TYPE, e.g. AN ETHICS APPLICATION FOR HUMAN/ANIMAL WORK] at [INSTITUTION]. List every document, form and annex typically required, grouped by category, with a checkbox for each. Add a final row: "verified against current guidance on [DATE] by [NAME]".
Get a first draft of a data-sharing agreement or MTA to react to
Draft a first-draft [DATA-SHARING AGREEMENT / MATERIAL TRANSFER AGREEMENT] between our lab and [PARTNER] for [PURPOSE, e.g. SHARING SEQUENCE DATA FROM A JOINT TRIAL]. Cover: parties, purpose, permitted use, attribution, term, and what happens at project end. At the very top add, in bold: "UNREVIEWED DRAFT — FOR INTERNAL DISCUSSION AND LEGAL REVIEW ONLY." Use plain language and mark anything to be negotiated as [TO BE NEGOTIATED].
Scan the landscape of methods or tools for a task
Act as a methods analyst. Give me a landscape scan of tools/methods for [TASK, e.g. AMPLICON DENOISING] as used in [FIELD / SYSTEM]: main options, typical use case, and known limitations. Be explicit about what you don't know — put uncertain or unverifiable points under "LOW CONFIDENCE — VERIFY" rather than stating them as fact.

5 Project Management, Data & Reporting

Meeting minutes, progress-report sections, first-pass analysis and status updates — with owners, dates and reasoning you can actually check.

Turn a meeting transcript into minutes, decisions and actions
From this meeting transcript, produce three things: [PASTE TRANSCRIPT] 1. Concise minutes (key discussion points). 2. A list of decisions made. 3. An action table: Action · Owner · Due date. Do not invent owners or dates — if the transcript does not say who or when, write "TBC". Keep any caveats raised (e.g. "this result is preliminary") attached to the relevant action, not dropped.
Draft a progress-report section from your data
You are a research assistant helping with reporting. Write the "Progress Against Plan" section of a progress report for [PROJECT] using this data: [PASTE INDICATORS / RESULTS]. For each indicator: target, actual, and a one-line explanation of variance. Use only the figures provided. Flag any indicator with missing data as "data pending".
Get a first-pass read on a dataset — with reasoning shown
Do a first-pass analysis of this data: [PASTE DATA / ATTACH]. Tell me the main patterns, any outliers, and 3 questions worth investigating further. Show your reasoning step by step so I can check how you reached each conclusion. Do not treat correlation as cause. This is a first look, not a final analysis.
Write a weekly status report from bullet updates
Turn these rough updates into a weekly status report for [PROJECT / TEAM]: [PASTE BULLETS]. Sections: Done this week · In progress · Blockers · Next week. Keep it tight — a supervisor should read it in under a minute. Flag blockers clearly.
Standardise reports that everyone formats differently
Here are field reports from three sites in different formats: [PASTE / ATTACH]. Rewrite all of them into this single standard template: [PASTE YOUR TEMPLATE HEADINGS]. Preserve every fact and figure exactly. Where a site omitted a required field, write "Not reported".

6 Knowledge Management & Everyday Productivity

Fast document triage, email drafting, onboarding help, and getting unstuck when you don't know how to start.

Get the "so what do I do?" from a long document
Read this document and give me an "act on this" summary: [PASTE / ATTACH]. Return four short sections: Executive summary (3 lines) · Decisions I need to make · Deadlines & dates · Questions I should ask. Skip everything that needs no action from me.
Draft the same email in two tones so you can pick
Draft an email to [RECIPIENT] about [SUBJECT / GOAL]. Key points: [WHAT MUST BE SAID]. Give me two versions: (A) warm and relationship-first, (B) brief and direct. Keep both under 150 words with a clear subject line and one specific ask.
Create onboarding FAQs from your own material
Using this material, write an onboarding FAQ for a new [ROLE, e.g. HONOURS STUDENT] joining the lab: [PASTE POLICIES / NOTES]. 10–12 question-and-answer pairs a new joiner would actually ask, plain language. Only use information in the material — mark anything you cannot answer as "Ask your supervisor".
When you're stuck and don't even know what to ask
I need to [TASK] but I'm stuck. Ask me 5 questions that would help you help me, then wait for my answers.
This one prompt fixes most "blank page" moments

Instead of forcing a bad first prompt, let the AI interview you. Answer its questions and the draft it produces next will be far closer to what you actually need.

Card F

Check before you trust

The card you use before anything leaves your desk.

List every factual claim, number and citation in your answer, and tell me which I must independently verify and which you're confident about.
Before you hit send: the verification habit

AI gives you speed. You provide the judgement. Every number, name, citation, deadline and ethics or regulatory claim is yours to confirm before it reaches a supervisor, a journal, a funder, or a collaborator. Use Card F on anything that matters, mark AI drafts as drafts, and never let a confident-sounding sentence replace a fact you can check.
